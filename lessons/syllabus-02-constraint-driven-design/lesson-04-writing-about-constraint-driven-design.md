# Lesson 02.04: Writing About Constraint-Driven Design

**Parent syllabus:** [Syllabus 02: Constraint-Driven Design](../../syllabus-02-constraint-driven-design.md)  
**Estimated time:** 2 hours  
**Artifact:** Publishable essay outline

---

## Outcome

By the end of this lesson, you can explain constraint-driven design in writing without making the piece too abstract, too personal, or too salesy.

You will produce a publishable essay outline and opening section.

---

## Why This Matters

Writing is how your philosophy becomes legible.

Common failures:

- The essay explains the idea but gives no proof.
- The piece overshares personal health details before establishing design value.
- The writing sounds like a manifesto instead of a useful framework.
- The reader cannot tell how to apply the idea.
- The essay does not connect to the kind of work you want to be hired for.

Good writing turns lived practice into a transferable design method.

---

## Best-Practice Principles

1. **Lead with the design problem.**  
   Start with the failure your reader recognizes.

2. **Define the philosophy early.**  
   Do not make the reader wait for the core idea.

3. **Use personal context with control.**  
   Share only what strengthens the point and only at the depth you choose.

4. **Make examples concrete.**  
   Show a before-and-after workflow, not only principles.

5. **Teach a method.**  
   The reader should leave with a checklist, questions, or a pattern they can use.

6. **Connect to business and human value.**  
   Constraint-driven design improves adoption, reliability, safety, and maintainability.

7. **End with a practical takeaway.**  
   Avoid vague inspiration. Give the reader a next action.

---

## Concepts

### Reader Promise

What the reader will understand or be able to do after reading.

### Controlled Disclosure

Choosing how much personal context to include, rather than letting the story become the whole argument.

### Transferable Frame

A concept the reader can apply outside your personal situation.

### Proof Example

A concrete story showing the principle in action.

---

## Essay Structure

Use this outline:

```markdown
# Working Title

## Opening Problem

Describe a common design failure.

## Definition

Define constraint-driven design in one sentence.

## Why Constraints Improve Design

Explain why limits force clarity, defaults, handoffs, and maintainable systems.

## Example 1

Before:
After:
Design principle:

## Example 2

Before:
After:
Design principle:

## How to Apply It

Give the reader 5-7 questions.

## What This Changes

Explain the business and user value.

## Closing

Give one practical next action.
```

## Walkthrough

Weak opening:

> I have MS, so I think a lot about designing around limitations.

This may be true, but it starts with personal context before the reader understands the design frame.

Stronger opening:

> Most workflows are designed for ideal conditions: full attention, enough time, a clear next step, and a user who remembers where everything lives. Real work rarely looks like that. Constraint-driven design starts from the opposite assumption: the system should still work when attention, time, energy, tools, or risk tolerance are limited.

Why it works:

- It starts with a recognizable design failure.
- It does not require personal disclosure.
- It defines the method.
- It applies broadly.

Personal context can come later:

> I came to this philosophy through necessity, but it has become one of my strongest design filters. Systems that work under constraint are usually clearer, safer, and easier for everyone else to use too.

### Application questions

Use these in the essay:

- What does this workflow assume about the user's energy?
- What does it assume the user remembers?
- What decision can be turned into a safe default?
- What output can be made smaller?
- Where should the system stop instead of pushing forward?
- Where does human review belong?
- How does the workflow resume after interruption?

## Practice

Write a publishable essay outline.

Include:

1. Working title
2. Reader promise
3. Opening problem
4. One-sentence definition
5. Two proof examples
6. Application checklist
7. Business value paragraph
8. Closing next action

Then write the opening 300-500 words.

---

## Review Checklist

Your essay outline is acceptable when:

- The reader promise is clear.
- Constraint-driven design is defined early.
- The piece does not depend entirely on personal disclosure.
- At least two concrete examples are included.
- The reader gets a reusable checklist or method.
- Business value is explicit.
- The tone is clear and practical.
- The closing gives a next action.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **Manifesto without method:** The piece argues but does not teach.
- **Oversharing by accident:** Personal context takes over the design point.
- **Too abstract:** The reader cannot picture the workflow.
- **No business connection:** Clients cannot tell why it matters.
- **Too defensive:** The piece explains why constraints are valid instead of showing how they improve systems.
- **No examples:** The philosophy has no proof.
