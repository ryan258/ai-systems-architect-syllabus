# Lesson 02.05: Teaching This to a Client

**Parent syllabus:** [Syllabus 02: Constraint-Driven Design](../../syllabus-02-constraint-driven-design.md)  
**Estimated time:** 2 hours  
**Artifact:** Client explanation and objection-handling sheet

---

## Outcome

By the end of this lesson, you can explain constraint-driven design to a client as a business advantage: better adoption, fewer errors, less training burden, safer workflows, and systems that work under real conditions.

You will produce a client explanation, discovery questions, and answers to common objections.

---

## Why This Matters

Clients do not buy a philosophy. They buy outcomes.

Common failures:

- The explanation sounds personal but not commercially relevant.
- The client hears "extra accessibility work" instead of better system design.
- The concept sounds like slowing down instead of reducing rework.
- The business value is implied but not stated.
- Objections are handled defensively.

To sell constraint-driven design, you need to translate it into outcomes clients already care about.

---

## Best-Practice Principles

1. **Start with their pain.**  
   Adoption, errors, training, context switching, rework, and support burden are client problems.

2. **Use business language.**  
   Talk about throughput, reliability, risk, onboarding, and maintainability.

3. **Make it a feature, not a workaround.**  
   Constraint-driven design means fewer assumptions and better defaults.

4. **Show examples.**  
   Clients understand the value faster when they see before-and-after workflows.

5. **Ask diagnostic questions.**  
   Let the client discover where their current system assumes ideal conditions.

6. **Do not overclaim.**  
   Constraint-driven design reduces friction and risk. It does not guarantee adoption or eliminate training.

7. **Connect to implementation choices.**  
   Explain how the philosophy changes actual architecture: fewer steps, embedded tools, handoffs, defaults, checklists, and runbooks.

## Concepts

### Client Translation

Turning a design principle into client-relevant business language.

Example:

> "Low context switching" becomes "your team does not need to leave Slack to approve a draft."

### Objection

A reasonable concern the client may raise.

Examples:

- "Will this slow the team down?"
- "Why not automate the whole thing?"
- "Do we really need human review?"
- "Why not build a dashboard?"

### Diagnostic Question

A question that reveals the client's real constraint.

Example:

> "Where do users currently abandon the workflow?"

---

## Walkthrough

Weak explanation:

> I design around constraints because people have limited energy and attention.

Better client explanation:

> Constraint-driven design means we design the AI workflow around the real conditions your team works under: limited time, existing tools, interruptions, approval requirements, and uneven adoption. Instead of assuming users will remember a new process, we build the workflow into the places they already work, reduce unnecessary decisions, and add review points before risky actions.

### Business value translation

| Design principle | Client value |
| --- | --- |
| Fewer decisions | Faster adoption |
| Better defaults | Less training |
| Embedded workflow | Less context switching |
| Human review gates | Lower risk |
| Resume points | Less lost work |
| Plain-language handoff docs | Easier support and onboarding |

### Discovery questions

- Where does this workflow currently slow down?
- What do users forget to do?
- Where do mistakes become expensive?
- Which steps require approval?
- Which tools does the team already live in?
- What would make people avoid using a new system?
- What does a bad day look like for this team?

### Objection handling

Objection:

> "Why not just automate the whole workflow?"

Response:

> We can automate the low-risk parts first, but the high-impact steps need evidence and review. The goal is not maximum automation. The goal is reliable automation that the team can trust.

Objection:

> "Is this just accessibility work?"

Response:

> Accessibility is part of it, but the design frame is broader. We are reducing operational friction: fewer unnecessary decisions, clearer defaults, better handoffs, and workflows that fit the way the team already works.

Objection:

> "Will this slow people down?"

Response:

> It usually speeds up the repeated work because the system removes avoidable decisions. The review points are reserved for steps where mistakes would be more expensive than a pause.

## Practice

Create a client explanation kit:

1. **One-sentence explanation**
2. **One-paragraph explanation**
3. **Three business benefits**
4. **Five discovery questions**
5. **Three before-and-after examples**
6. **Three likely objections**
7. **Your answer to each objection**
8. **One proposal paragraph**

Use language a non-technical client can repeat.

---

## Review Checklist

Your client kit is acceptable when:

- It starts from client pain, not your philosophy.
- It explains business value.
- It includes concrete before-and-after examples.
- It avoids overclaiming.
- It connects to actual implementation choices.
- It includes discovery questions.
- It includes objection responses.
- It can be used in a proposal or sales conversation.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **Philosophy first:** The client cannot connect the concept to their business.
- **Too defensive:** The explanation argues for constraints instead of showing value.
- **No examples:** The client cannot picture the difference.
- **Automation maximalism:** The pitch implies more automation is always better.
- **No objections:** The first client concern derails the conversation.
- **Too technical:** The value is hidden behind architecture language.
