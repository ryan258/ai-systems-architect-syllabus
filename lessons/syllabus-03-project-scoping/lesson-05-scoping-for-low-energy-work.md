# Lesson 03.05: Scoping for Low-Energy Work

**Parent syllabus:** [Syllabus 03: Project Scoping](../../syllabus-03-project-scoping.md)  
**Estimated time:** 2 hours  
**Artifact:** Energy-aware scope policy

---

## Outcome

By the end of this lesson, you can write project scopes that protect realistic pacing without oversharing personal health details.

You will produce an energy-aware scope policy that defines async delivery, review windows, buffer time, communication boundaries, and handoff expectations.

---

## Why This Matters

Your scope is also your working environment.

If the scope assumes constant availability, same-day revisions, live calls for every decision, and unlimited context switching, the project will be fragile even if the technical work is good.

Common failures:

- You promise speed because you want to win the project.
- The client expects instant responses.
- Feedback arrives in scattered messages across multiple channels.
- You depend on live meetings for decisions that could be async.
- You hide buffer time instead of designing with it.
- A bad day turns into missed expectations instead of normal operating range.

Professional pacing is not an apology. It is part of delivery design.

---

## Best-Practice Principles

1. **Make async the default when possible.**  
   Async review protects focus and creates a written record.

2. **Use review windows, not constant availability.**  
   A defined feedback window is easier to honor than open-ended responsiveness.

3. **Build buffer into the schedule.**  
   Buffer is risk management, not dishonesty.

4. **Centralize communication.**  
   One project thread or document reduces context switching and lost decisions.

5. **Separate urgent from important.**  
   Most project decisions do not require same-hour response.

6. **Design handoffs for low-energy days.**  
   Templates, checklists, and documented defaults reduce memory load.

7. **Keep personal details optional.**  
   You can explain your process without disclosing health information.

## Concepts

### Operating Cadence

The expected rhythm of communication, review, and delivery.

Example:

> Project updates are sent twice per week. Feedback is collected in the shared scope document. Revisions are batched into scheduled review cycles.

### Review Window

A defined period where the client reviews a deliverable and returns consolidated feedback.

Example:

> Client feedback is due within three business days of draft delivery.

### Consolidated Feedback

Feedback collected into one place before revisions begin.

This prevents scattered requests from becoming hidden scope creep.

### Buffer

Time intentionally reserved for review, integration friction, access delays, or unexpected complexity.

Buffer should be included in the estimate rather than treated as a secret emergency reserve.

---

## Walkthrough

Weak scope language:

> I can probably turn this around quickly. Just send feedback whenever.

This creates unstable expectations. "Quickly" has no boundary. "Whenever" creates endless context switching.

Stronger scope language:

> Draft delivery is scheduled for Friday. Client feedback is collected in the shared document by the following Wednesday. One revision cycle is included and will be completed after consolidated feedback is received. Project communication will stay in email and the shared document unless we schedule a call.

### What this protects

| Risk | Scope protection |
| --- | --- |
| Scattered feedback | Shared document |
| Constant availability | Scheduled updates |
| Rushed revisions | Review window |
| Energy crashes | Buffer in timeline |
| Forgotten decisions | Written communication |
| Surprise calls | Scheduled calls only |

### Client-facing explanation

Use process language:

> I work best with a structured async process because it creates better documentation, fewer missed decisions, and cleaner revisions. You will always know what is due, when feedback is needed, and what the next delivery milestone is.

You do not need to say:

> I need this because of my health.

You can if you choose, but the scope should stand on professional process alone.

## Energy-Aware Scope Clauses

### Communication

> Project communication will happen through [channel]. To keep decisions clear, feedback and approvals should be placed in the shared project document or sent in one consolidated message.

### Updates

> I will provide project updates on [cadence], unless a blocker requires earlier notice.

### Feedback window

> Client feedback is due within [number] business days of draft delivery. Revision work begins after consolidated feedback is received.

### Calls

> Calls are scheduled in advance with a clear agenda. Decisions made on calls will be summarized in writing.

### Response time

> Standard response time is [number] business days. Urgent requests outside the agreed scope may require a separate agreement.

### Timeline buffer

> Timeline estimates include review, revision, access setup, and normal delivery buffer.

### Handoff

> Final delivery includes a written handoff so the client can operate the workflow without relying on live memory or unscheduled support.

## Practice

Write an energy-aware scope policy for your consulting work.

Include:

1. Communication channels
2. Response time
3. Update cadence
4. Review windows
5. Consolidated feedback rule
6. Call policy
7. Buffer policy
8. Revision timing
9. Handoff standard
10. Emergency or urgent request boundary

Then rewrite this weak statement:

> I am flexible and can work around your schedule.

Turn it into professional scope language that protects both sides.

---

## Review Checklist

Your energy-aware scope policy is acceptable when:

- It sounds like a professional operating process, not an apology.
- It does not require personal health disclosure.
- It defines communication channels.
- It defines response expectations.
- It uses review windows.
- It requires consolidated feedback.
- It includes timeline buffer.
- It reduces context switching.
- It supports clean handoff.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **Oversharing as a substitute for scope:** Personal context does not replace clear process.
- **No buffer:** The first access delay breaks the schedule.
- **Too many channels:** Decisions are scattered across email, text, Slack, calls, and documents.
- **No review window:** Feedback arrives indefinitely.
- **Live-call dependency:** Every decision requires high-energy synchronous work.
- **Hidden availability assumptions:** The client expects immediate response because the scope never says otherwise.
