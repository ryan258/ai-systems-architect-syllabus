# Syllabus 19: AI Governance and Risk
**Status:** Enterprise Gap - Knowing When Not to Automate
**Format:** One chat session per unit, self-paced

---

## What This Is

AI systems do not only create technical risk. They create business, legal, ethical, operational, and reputation risk. This syllabus teaches you how to identify those risks, document them clearly, choose mitigations, and decide when the safest architecture is less automation.

---

## Unit 1 - AI Risk Management Foundations

**Goal:** Understand AI risk as part of system design, not a compliance afterthought.

**Topics:**
- What AI governance means in plain language
- Risk categories: harm, bias, privacy, security, reliability, cost, and misuse
- NIST AI Risk Management Framework concepts
- Govern, map, measure, and manage as a practical review loop
- How governance differs for internal tools, client tools, and public tools

**Session starter:**
> "Teach me AI governance and risk management for practical client systems. Explain the NIST AI Risk Management Framework in plain language and show me how to turn it into a review process I can actually use."

---

## Unit 2 - Risk Registers and Mitigation Plans

**Goal:** Track AI system risks in a way clients and operators can understand.

**Topics:**
- What a risk register is
- Severity, likelihood, owner, mitigation, and residual risk
- Turning vague concerns into concrete risks
- Deciding which risks block launch
- Reviewing risks after real usage begins

**Session starter:**
> "Help me build a risk register for an AI system. Show me how to write concrete risks, score severity and likelihood, assign owners, define mitigations, and decide which risks block launch."

---

## Unit 3 - Model Cards, Data Cards, and Audit Evidence

**Goal:** Document what the system uses and why, without pretending to know more than you do.

**Topics:**
- Model cards in plain language
- Data cards for client datasets and retrieval corpora
- Documenting model selection, known limits, and fallback plans
- Keeping audit evidence: tests, evals, access reviews, and incident history
- Explaining uncertainty honestly to clients

**Session starter:**
> "Teach me model cards, data cards, and audit evidence for AI systems. I need lightweight templates that explain model choice, data sources, known limits, evals, incidents, and human oversight."

---

## Unit 4 - Human Accountability and Approval Design

**Goal:** Make sure the system has a clear human owner for important decisions.

**Topics:**
- Decisions AI should recommend vs. decisions AI should execute
- Approval gates for high-impact actions
- Escalation paths and human ownership
- Designing review screens that reduce rubber-stamping
- Avoiding overreliance on plausible model output

**Session starter:**
> "Help me design human accountability for an AI system. Which actions should require approval? How do I avoid rubber-stamp reviews? How do I make sure a real person owns high-impact decisions?"

---

## Unit 5 - Vendor, Policy, and Compliance Readiness

**Goal:** Know what to ask vendors and what policies a client may need before deployment.

**Topics:**
- Vendor risk questions for model providers, vector databases, and cloud platforms
- Data retention, training use, access controls, and breach notification
- Internal AI use policies
- When to involve legal, security, or compliance professionals
- Writing plain-language governance notes for a proposal

**Session starter:**
> "Help me create a vendor and policy readiness checklist for AI projects. What should I ask model providers, cloud providers, and vector database vendors about data retention, training use, access controls, and incidents?"

---

## Practice Project

Choose one AI system idea. Write a risk register with at least ten risks, a model card, a data card, an approval-gate plan, and a one-page client-facing governance note that explains what the system will and will not do.
