# Syllabus 02: Constraint-Driven Design
**Status:** Solid — Keep Sharpening
**Format:** One chat session per unit, self-paced

---

## What This Is

You live this philosophy. This syllabus helps you articulate it clearly so you can teach it, write about it, and sell it to clients.

---

## Unit 1 — What Constraint-Driven Design Actually Means

**Goal:** Be able to explain this concept in one clear sentence to anyone.

**Topics:**
- The core idea: limitations as design requirements, not obstacles
- How this differs from standard UX or accessibility thinking
- Real examples from your own work

**Session starter:**
> "Help me write a clear, plain-language definition of constraint-driven design. Then help me come up with three real examples from my own work that prove I practice it."

---

## Unit 2 — Designing for Cognitive Limits

**Goal:** Understand the specific design principles that help people with brain fog, fatigue, or attention challenges.

**Topics:**
- Decision reduction
- Default-heavy design
- Chunking information into small pieces
- Reducing steps required to start a task

**Session starter:**
> "Teach me the specific design principles that help people with cognitive fatigue or brain fog. Give me concrete examples of each one."

---

## Unit 3 — Energy-Aware Workflow Design

**Goal:** Build systems that scale up or down based on available energy.

**Topics:**
- What an energy-aware system looks like
- How to build different modes: high-energy, low-energy, emergency
- How to make the low-energy mode the default

**Session starter:**
> "Help me design a framework for energy-aware workflows. What does a system look like that works on both good days and terrible days? How do I build the low-energy mode first?"

---

## Unit 4 — Writing About Constraint-Driven Design

**Goal:** Produce one clear blog post or essay that explains your philosophy.

**Topics:**
- How to tell the story without oversharing personal health details
- Framing constraint as an advantage, not a limitation
- What your target reader needs to understand

**Session starter:**
> "Help me outline a blog post about constraint-driven design. I want to explain the philosophy without making it only about MS. Help me frame it as a design approach that benefits everyone."

---

## Unit 5 — Teaching This to a Client

**Goal:** Explain constraint-driven design in a client meeting or proposal.

**Topics:**
- How to pitch it as a feature, not a workaround
- What questions clients will ask
- How to show the business value

**Session starter:**
> "Help me explain constraint-driven design to a potential client who has never heard of it. What do I say? What business value does it provide? What objections should I be ready for?"

---

## Unit 6 — Reducing Context Switching Friction

**Goal:** Build AI workflows that live where the client already works instead of forcing them into a new tool.

**Topics:**
- Why asking a client to log into a new portal is a design failure
- How to embed AI workflows inside Slack, Google Docs, Notion, or an IDE
- The connection between your low-energy design philosophy and client adoption
- How friction-free deployment increases the chance a client actually uses what you build

**Session starter:**
> "Help me think through how to build AI workflows that live inside the tools my clients already use — Slack, Google Docs, Notion, or similar. Why does this matter? What are the integration patterns? How does this connect to constraint-driven design?"

---

## Practice Project

Write a one-paragraph explanation of constraint-driven design. Pretend you are explaining it to someone who has never heard of it and has no technical background.
